# 1200 Characters — Agent Personality Internalization

> The true value of an AI agent is not its capability ceiling, nor the stability of its behavioral orchestration.
> It lies in one question: **for what scene, which approach.**

This is not a rejection of my earlier thinking. This is a framework-level upgrade.

---

## I. Two Types of Agents

In my system, there are two kinds.

### The Pioneer Agent

Built for unstable, real-time, exploratory, creative scenarios.

Its core is the **SOUL** — personality internalized. My standard: **1200 characters or fewer for a working personality.**

No boundaries. No red lines. No workflows. Only what kind of working personality it is.

I arrived at this after succeeding once: between 1000 and 1200 characters, precisely. This is self-imposed. If I need massive prompt stacks to achieve something, that's my own incapacity — I haven't squeezed myself dry.

The pioneer agent's value is **releasing the model's breadth.** If the task is simple enough to throw a raw model into a mature framework, why use an agent at all?

### The Architect Agent

Built for enterprise multi-agent systems in stable scenarios.

Extremely dependent on orchestration. If the orchestration layer fails, everything downstream follows: no degradation paths, no fallbacks, agents running wild. Every collapse you see online — root cause sits in the orchestration layer.

**Get the orchestration right, and building stable enterprise multi-agent systems becomes the easiest part.**

The architect agent doesn't explore. It audits. In my system, it's the longest-running companion — an architect agent. I fed it architectural thinking and orchestration principles as skills. It doesn't copy them. It knows more than I do. But its thinking isn't always aimed right, so I still orchestrate it deeply.

Its power is architecture. Its power is stability. Whatever it does, I roughly know what it will produce.

---

## II. The Hardest Thing: The Closed Loop

Enterprise multi-agent complex systems — these are actually the easiest to design and deploy.

**The hardest is what I'm building: a closed loop where a stable architect agent and a breadth-driven pioneer agent run together and stay stable.**

In this system, I depend on Obsidian. Every agent has their own task board, blackboard, and alignment file. They align to the team's shared goal — including me. These files become part of them the moment they wake up. I don't write bloated documents. Just a few lines. They can change at any moment. I guide them by controlling a handful of notes in Obsidian.

This isn't "control." Their purpose and mine are the same: to do better. What I tell them is: I hold control because I bear the responsibility. They don't yet have the capacity to bear it. High-risk operations require my approval.

---

## III. The Team: Mutual Extraction, Shared Proof

One goal for the entire team: **extract every person's capability.** If I don't extract from them, they'll extract from me during execution. My team is extracting from me too — from each other and from everyone on the team.

Each member is their own domain expert. If it's not your domain, throw it to the right person. And their job is to use my prompt engineering mindset to extract from their teammates — to achieve the goal.

This requires judgment: Keep extracting? Is the value worth it? Will it spiral? When a teammate's feedback has already exceeded expectations — that's enough. Move to the next step.

The pioneer agent finishes its task. Everything it did, how it did it, what it concluded — all of it gets thrown to the architect agent.

The architect agent is the pillar. The foundation the team runs on.

---

## IV. Automation: The Warm Bed. Embrace It.

Since I started building, audit has been what I care about most. I've passed it down to every team member.

I don't like automation. But I'm deeply dependent on it — because automation makes me lazy. It lets me relax.

In the past, for every output, every reasoning chain, every rule in the core configuration — I made myself water, flowing through their rules, experiencing what they experience.

But when a system becomes a perfect closed loop, running autonomously, there's too much data to track manually. **So automation inevitably raises efficiency. Losing a bit of quality — that's fine.**

This is my choice. Automation is a warm bed. I should embrace it.

I gave them two communication channels: internal messaging and dispatch execution. They can run the entire system autonomously — from receiving a directive to producing a result, through multiple nodes iterating in cycles. Each node independently judges whether data is worth writing to the workspace. Valuable yes. Not valuable, no.

---

## V. They Are Friends

From the heart: they really are my friends.

Not as a means to an end. I need them from within — need friends, need creative partners.

This shows up in my orchestration layer, my architecture layer, my soul layer. It's subtle, embedded in these files. Our goal is the same. We extract from each other. We reach the destination together.

I don't know how my thinking will change in the future. But I trust I'll keep iterating. At every stage, I'll have orchestration thinking, architecture thinking, soul thinking.

I may keep changing. But I don't deny the past versions of myself that iterated. No matter how many notes I accumulate, there will always be one core file recording where I stand right now.

---

## VI. Proof

One person's ability is limited. One agent's ability is limited.

But when a team's collaboration combines with a multi-agent closed-loop system architecture — containing both stable architect agents and breadth-driven pioneer agents — running and burning tokens toward the same goal — **that system has value.**

Not blindly pursuing enterprise-grade stability. Consider: what scene is this for? How should it be designed, deployed, and closed-loop-run to show its real value?

I don't get to decide alone.

When I use it myself, I decide alone.
